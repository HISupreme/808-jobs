Becoming a Hawaii resident

Six things to do, in order, mostly within 30 days. Hawaii law gives you 30 days from the date residency is established to register your vehicle and exchange your driver license. The home exemption deadline is separate (Sept 30 in Honolulu, Dec 31 in other counties).

  1. Establish your address. Sign a lease, close on a home, or move into an existing residence. Save two pieces of mail addressed to you at the new address — utility bills, lease agreement, bank statements. You'll need them for almost every following step.
  2. Within 30 days, exchange your out-of-state driver license for a Hawaii license. Apply in person at any county driver licensing center with proof of identity, SSN, and two proofs of residency. If you have a license from another state, surrender it on the spot. → Driver License Application.
    If you want REAL ID: apply for it at the same time — same trip, same documents, gold star added. → REAL ID Upgrade. After May 7, 2025 enforcement, REAL ID is required for domestic flights and federal facility access.
  3. If you brought a vehicle from the mainland, register it within 30 days of arrival. Pass a Hawaii safety inspection first ($15-$25), then bring the certificate, your out-of-state title, your Hawaii license, proof of Hawaii insurance, and the bill of lading or shipping receipt to your county DMV. You cannot register online for an initial out-of-state transfer; it must be in person. → Out-of-State Vehicle Registration (Newcomer) · → Periodic Vehicle Safety Inspection.
    Active-duty military exception: Form CS-L(MVR)50 lets you keep out-of-state registration while stationed in Hawaii — but you still need a passing safety inspection and Hawaii-compliant insurance.
  4. Register to vote. Hawaii uses a permanent vote-by-mail system — once registered, ballots arrive automatically before each election. The fastest path is the Online Voter Registration System (OLVR), which uses your Hawaii driver license number. → Voter Registration.
  5. If you bought a home, file the home exemption claim with your county Real Property Assessment office. Saves $500-$2,000+ per year. The Honolulu deadline is September 30; other counties use December 31. File once — it auto-renews until you sell, move, or rent out the property.
    Pick the right county: → Honolulu · → Hawai'i Island · → Maui · → Kaua'i.
  6. Update your address everywhere else. SSA (online at ssa.gov), IRS (Form 8822), USPS (mail forwarding), employer for state withholding (HW-4), and any banks or insurance companies. If you've already registered your vehicle in Hawaii, the DMV needs an address-change notice for vehicle records too. → Vehicle Registration Address Change.

What people get wrong

The most expensive miss is the home exemption. The deadline lands quietly six to nine months after most people close on a Hawaii home — long after the move-in dust has settled. About 80,000 Oahu owner-occupants are estimated to have never filed for it, surrendering an estimated $30 million in property-tax savings every year. Filing once is enough; the exemption renews automatically. If you bought between October 1, 2025 and September 30, 2026 on Oahu, your deadline is September 30, 2026.

The second most expensive miss is the 30-day vehicle window. Hawaii's late-registration penalty starts at $16 per vehicle and accrues annually — a small dollar amount, but a citation during a traffic stop can compound into much more. The clock starts when residency is established, not when the vehicle arrives. Both your DL and registration are 30-day deadlines.

If you're moving from California, Oregon, Washington, or another state with strong consumer-protection rules, expect Hawaii's process to feel paper-heavy. Most initial transactions cannot be done online and require an in-person visit. Book DMV appointments at AlohaQ.org for Honolulu; other counties have their own systems. Allow most of a morning per visit.

Buying or selling a home in Hawaii

Most paperwork is handled by escrow and title — but the post-closing steps are yours. The forms below are the ones that hit your bottom line: HARPTA withholding, conveyance tax, home exemption. Miss them and you leave thousands of dollars unclaimed or send thousands extra to the state.

  1. Open escrow. Your real estate agent and the title/escrow company drive most of the standard paperwork: purchase contract, disclosures, title search, lender documents. As a buyer or seller, you'll sign — but the forms below are the ones that need attention beyond signing.
  2. If selling and you're a Hawaii resident: file Form N-289 with escrow to certify Hawaii residency and avoid HARPTA withholding entirely. If you're a non-resident or claim ambiguous residency, escrow withholds 7.25% of the gross sale price under HARPTA. → Form N-289 — HARPTA Exemption Certification.
    Non-resident selling at a loss or low gain? File Form N-288C (tentative refund) after closing, with documentation of basis (original closing statement, capital improvement records). Faster than waiting until the next January's N-15. Hawaii will scrutinize the claim — keep records meticulously.
  3. At closing, conveyance tax is paid via Form P-64A (or P-64B if exempt). Escrow handles the form and payment. The seller is generally responsible for paying — though it's negotiable. Verify that the buyer's home-exemption eligibility box is checked on P-64A line 5 if the buyer plans to claim the home exemption — it changes the rate from $0.20/$100 to $0.10/$100 on a sub-$600K residential sale, a real savings on closing costs. → Forms P-64A & P-64B — Conveyance Tax.
  4. If you sold your principal residence, report it on next year's tax return: Form N-103 reports the sale of a personal residence. Most sellers qualify for the IRC §121 exclusion — up to $250,000 of gain ($500,000 married filing jointly) is tax-free if you owned and lived in the home for 2 of the last 5 years. → Form N-103 — Sale of Home.
  5. If you bought, file the home exemption with your county RPAD by deadline. Honolulu: September 30. Other counties: December 31. The deadline applies to the year following purchase — file once, it auto-renews. → Honolulu · → Hawai'i Island · → Maui · → Kaua'i.
  6. Update your address with everyone else. Lender (mortgage statements), insurance (homeowners and any other), USPS forwarding, DOTAX (Form ITPS-COA), employer (HW-4), DMV if you also moved your vehicle to a different county, and the IRS (Form 8822).

For sellers: the HARPTA distinction

HARPTA is not a tax — it's a withholding. Hawaii holds 7.25% of the sale price as a security deposit until the seller files a Hawaii income tax return showing the actual capital gains owed. If the actual liability is less than withheld, you get a refund. If you have no gain at all, you can recover the entire withholding — but you have to file the paperwork. N-289 (residence certification) eliminates withholding upfront for residents. N-288B (waiver application) reduces it for non-residents who can show they'll have minimal gain. N-288C (tentative refund) recovers withheld funds before the next year's tax filing season.

For buyers: the conveyance-tax math

Conveyance tax rates climb steeply. On residential properties under $600K where the buyer will claim the home exemption: $0.10 per $100 of sale price ($1,000 on a $1M sale would be wrong — it's actually only on the portion that triggers, but the rate logic is bracketed). On non-resident investor purchases over $10M with no home exemption: $1.25 per $100. The certification check on P-64A line 5 is the buyer's job to verify before signing — escrow does the math, but you sign under penalty of perjury. Foreign buyers face FIRPTA (federal 15% withholding) on top of HARPTA — coordinate with title and a CPA.

What people get wrong

The most expensive seller miss is failing to file N-289 when you're actually a Hawaii resident — escrow then withholds HARPTA from the sale, and you wait months for the refund. The most expensive buyer miss is failing to file the home exemption by the next September 30 (Honolulu) or December 31 (other counties) — every year you miss costs $500-$2,000+. Both are forms you sign at closing or shortly after; both are worth a calendar reminder.

Starting a business in Hawaii

Five forms in roughly this order. Hawaii doesn't have a state-level general business license, but you do need a tax ID and a General Excise Tax license. Below is the path for a single-member LLC or simple corporation; sole proprietors can skip step 2.

  1. Choose your entity type. Sole proprietor (no formal entity, you and the business are the same), LLC (liability protection, pass-through tax, $51 to form), C-Corp (full liability shield, double taxation, $50 to form), or S-Corp (C-Corp that elected pass-through tax via IRS Form 2553). Most small Hawaii businesses choose LLC. The 808forms team is not a tax attorney; consult one if your business has investors, employees, or any complexity beyond owner-operated.
    If you already have an LLC and want to convert to a C-Corp later, you'll file Articles of Conversion (Form X-10) alongside Form DC-1 — but you don't need to think about that now.
  2. If LLC or Corp, register with DCCA. File Articles of Organization (LLC, $51) or Articles of Incorporation (C-Corp, $50) with the DCCA Business Registration Division. As of April 27, 2026, the new BREG portal handles all filings (Hawaii Business Express was retired). Standard processing: 10-15 business days. Expedited: $25 extra, 1-3 days. → Form LLC-1 (LLC Articles of Organization) · → Form DC-1 (C-Corp Articles of Incorporation).
    If using a name different from your legal entity name: register a Trade Name (DBA) with DCCA. → Form T-1 — Trade Name Registration.
  3. Get a federal EIN from the IRS. Free, online, takes 15 minutes at irs.gov. You'll need this for the Hawaii BB-1 application and for opening a business bank account. Sole proprietors can use their SSN, but most banks require an EIN for a business account anyway.
  4. File Form BB-1 to register with DOTAX and obtain your General Excise Tax license. $20 one-time fee. Hawaii's GET applies to almost everything (services, retail, rentals, freelance income) — at 4% on Oahu, Kauai, and Hawaii Island it includes a 0.5% county surcharge bringing it to 4.5%. Online via Hawaii Tax Online (5-7 days) or paper (4-6 weeks). → Form BB-1 — Basic Business Application.
    If you'll have employees: the BB-1 also covers withholding tax registration. Otherwise, you can register withholding later on a separate BB-1.
  5. Once operating, you're on the GET filing cycle. Periodic returns are Form G-45 (monthly if liability over $4,000/year, quarterly if over $2,000, semi-annual if under $2,000). Annual reconciliation is Form G-49, due April 20 of the following year. File and pay through Hawaii Tax Online.
    If you have employees: Form HW-4 collects withholding allowances from each employee on hire. Periodic withholding returns are Form HW-14; annual transmittal is Form HW-30. You'll also need to register for unemployment insurance with DLIR via Form UC-1.

The General Excise Tax surprise

The GET is what catches most new Hawaii business owners. It's not a sales tax — it's a tax on gross receipts (revenue before any expenses), and it applies to services, not just goods. A consultant earning $100K in revenue with $80K in expenses and $20K in profit pays GET on the full $100K, not on the $20K profit. Most businesses pass it to customers as a separate line item on invoices, which legally requires a 4.166% markup (because the tax also applies to the markup). If you don't pass it through, you absorb it. Either way, it's a real cost that erodes margin in low-margin businesses.

Things 808forms does not handle

Industry-specific licenses (real estate, contractors, food service, child care, beauty, professional services) are issued by separate DCCA boards or specialty agencies and are not covered above. The DCCA Business Action Center maintains a directory; check there before opening. County-level permits (zoning, signage, food handler) are also separate and county-specific. If you're employing minors, opening a regulated business, or operating in healthcare, education, or finance, the licensing layer is significantly larger than what's described here.

What people get wrong

Two common mistakes: not getting the GET license before starting to invoice (you can be assessed the tax even if you didn't collect it), and missing the Form G-49 annual reconciliation (it's separate from your periodic G-45 returns and easy to forget). For free, confidential help with small business setup and ongoing tax compliance, contact the Hawaii Small Business Development Center (SBDC) — they're free and will walk you through the process in plain language without charging.

The case for a working forms finder

Why this matters.

Hawaii residents leave tens of millions of dollars unclaimed every year and absorb measurable harm — eviction, lost coverage, lost ancestral land, sometimes lives — because government forms are hard to find, hard to read, and easy to miss. This is the evidence, with citations.

Hawaii has not built a forms infrastructure that meets its residents where they are. Forms are scattered across dozens of agency websites. PDFs are renamed every revision. Deadlines are buried. The state's official form finder lists five forms, none of which a regular resident would search for. The pattern is not unique to any one agency — it appears across property tax, worker benefits, healthcare, housing, courts, and Native Hawaiian programs. Each failure has a measurable cost.

What follows documents that cost. Every figure links to its primary source.

Money left on the table

$15.3 million in Hawaii's refundable food/excise tax credit went unclaimed in tax year 2021 alone — by 157,953 eligible residents, of whom 140,166 never filed a tax return at all. Source: Moore (UHERO) & Karacaovali (DOTAX), April 2024 — Unclaimed: Over $15 million in tax credits for low-income households

That is one form, one credit, one year. The credit's maximum per qualified exemption was doubled in 2023, so the foregone amount is now larger. A family of four below the income threshold can claim up to $880 a year. Most never file because they make too little to be required to file an income tax return — and the refundable credit is invisible to them.

~80,000 Oahu owner-occupant homeowners are estimated to have never filed for the home exemption — surrendering an estimated $30 million-plus in tax savings annually. Honolulu has roughly 285,000 residential parcels; only about 155,000 carry the homeowner exemption. Source: Hawaii Business Magazine analysis of RPAD and U.S. Census data, September 2022 — Homeowners' Omissions Provide More than $30 Million to Honolulu Treasury

The Honolulu home exemption removes $120,000 of assessed value from your property's tax basis ($160,000 if you're 65 or older). The deadline is September 30. You only have to file once. Yet about 38% of eligible owner-occupants on Oahu have not.

The neighbor islands are opaque on this question. None of the four counties publishes a count of eligible-versus-granted home exemptions. We don't know how many Big Island, Maui, or Kaua'i homeowners are leaving the same money on the table.

The form you're not allowed to download

Hawaii's TDI-45 form — the gateway to up to $22,646 in temporary disability benefits — is not posted online by the state. The Disability Compensation Division's official guidance is: "TDI-45 Claim for Disability Benefit forms are not available online. Ask your employer for a claim form." Source: Hawaii DLIR Disability Compensation Division — DCD Forms page

This is not an oversight. It's the design. The form has three parts: claimant, employer, doctor. It's mailed to the employer's private TDI insurance carrier — not to the state. The 90-day filing deadline is strict. The 26-week absolute bar is unforgiving. Hawaii TDI law does not, by DCD's own admission, "provide a specific timeframe within which the employer must complete Part B." A worker out of work for surgery has 90 days. The employer has none.

At the 2026 weekly maximum of $871 for up to 26 weeks, a single missed claim represents up to $22,646 in forfeited benefits. California's equivalent program, SDI, has been filed online directly with the state since the 2010s. Hawaii has not made the same change.

The Medicaid renewal you didn't know was coming

Roughly 25 million Americans lost Medicaid coverage during the 2023–2024 unwinding. 69% of disenrollments were procedural — paperwork failures, missed deadlines, wrong forms — not findings of ineligibility. Source: KFF Medicaid Enrollment and Unwinding Tracker, September 2024 — kff.org/medicaid

In Hawaii, Med-QUEST renewal arrives in a pink envelope — a Medical Eligibility Renewal Form. Miss the deadline and coverage ends. Net Hawaii Medicaid enrollment fell about 62,000 during unwinding; only 5,152 transitioned to a HealthCare.gov Marketplace plan. The vast majority of those who lost coverage did not enroll in alternative coverage.

Peer-reviewed research links Medicaid coverage discontinuity to a roughly 39% increase in emergency-department visits among adults with major depression. CMS in September 2023 ordered 30 states to reinstate roughly 500,000 individuals — most of them children — terminated through faulty processing. Hawaii's specific procedural-versus-ineligible split has not been publicly released.

What the homelessness numbers mean

Hawaii has the highest homelessness rate in the United States: 805 homeless residents per 100,000 in 2024 PIT counts — more than three times the national average. Roughly half of Hawaii eviction cases end in default judgment because the tenant fails to appear. Approximately 70% of landlords appear with counsel; only 5% of tenants do. Sources: HUD AHAR via Visual Capitalist — U.S. States With the Highest Homelessness Rates; Lawyers for Equal Justice — Evicted in Hawai'i

Eviction in Hawaii proceeds via summary possession under HRS Chapter 666. The state does not require a written Answer. Hearings often last fewer than two minutes. Eviction is the final outcome in 85–95% of cases. The peer-reviewed national evidence shows eviction filing alone — even without a judgment — is associated with a roughly 19% increase in mortality risk; an actual eviction with about 40%.

Act 278, signed in July 2025 and effective February 5, 2026, extended the nonpayment notice period from 5 business days to 10 calendar days and created a pre-filing mediation pilot. Under Hawaii's Act 57 precedent, pre-litigation mediation kept 85% of tenants housed versus 11% under post-filing mediation. The forms exist. Whether tenants find them in time is a different question.

Native Hawaiian programs and the cost of waiting

More than 2,000 Native Hawaiians have died on the Department of Hawaiian Home Lands waitlist without receiving a homestead lease. ProPublica calls the figure "almost certainly conservative." At the development pace of the prior 25 years, clearing the waitlist would take 182 more years. Source: ProPublica / Honolulu Star-Advertiser, "Promised Land" series — To Reclaim Ancestral Land

The DHHL waitlist holds roughly 28,700 unique applicants. Average wait time is about 19 years. If a waitlist applicant dies without filing the Designation of Successor form, the family has 180 days to claim the seat under HAR § 10-3-8 — after which the application is canceled and the family loses the place in line entirely. How many seats have been canceled this way is not publicly known.

The Kalima v. State class action settled for $328 million in July 2023. Of the roughly 2,515 qualifying class members, 1,164 had died before settlement. Their heirs are still navigating probate to receive payment.

808forms defers to the Office of Hawaiian Affairs, the Native Hawaiian Legal Corporation, and DHHL itself for any Native-Hawaiian-specific guidance. Cultural authority on these forms belongs to the community, not to a forms finder. We point. They lead.

The state's own form finder

Hawaii's official government form finder, at onlineforms.ehawaii.gov, lists 5 forms total: a driver record correction, a procurement award verification, an Oʻahu trail incident report, a neighbor-island court-volunteer signup, and the eHawaii subscriber agreement. None of the forms residents actually search for — home exemption, TDI claim, marriage license, Med-QUEST, voter registration, REAL ID, DHHL — are on it.

The site's Help page still displays lorem ipsum placeholder text in the FAQ ("Question_1? Nulla vitae elit libero, a pharetra augue…") with "department@agency.gov" as the contact email and "(808) 765-4321" as the phone number. The chat assistant declines to answer specific form questions. The technical-support line doesn't answer.

This is what the gap looks like. 808forms exists to fill it.

What the national evidence says

The administrative-burden frame published by Pamela Herd and Donald Moynihan (Russell Sage Foundation, 2018) treats forms not as administrative artifacts but as policy instruments: "Administrative burdens citizens regularly encounter in their interactions with the state are not simply unintended byproducts of governance, but the result of deliberate policy choices." Three costs disproportionately fall on the disadvantaged — learning costs, compliance costs, and psychological costs.

Where governments redesign forms around the user — Code for America's GetCalFresh raised California SNAP take-up from 66% to 81% by cutting application time from 45 minutes to under 10. CMS automation interventions across four states cut Medicaid procedural denials by 8.3 percentage points and increased ex parte renewals by 21.6 points (Health Affairs, Linke Young et al., 2025). The IRS's own published estimate is that about one in five eligible filers misses the federal Earned Income Tax Credit each year, leaving billions on the table.

The pattern is clean: when forms work, population statistics move. When they don't, residents lose money, coverage, custody, ancestral land, and sometimes their lives.

What we don't know yet

Several Hawaii-specific numbers that ought to be public are not. Each is a candidate for a public-records request, and each gap is itself a finding. Among them:

  • The four county Real Property Assessment offices do not publish counts of eligible-versus-granted home, disabled-veteran, or Kuleana exemptions.
  • DOTAX has applied its rigorous unclaimed-credit methodology to the food/excise credit but not to the renters credit or the state EITC after refundability.
  • The Disability Compensation Division does not publish TDI-45 claim volumes, denial reasons, or late-filing rates.
  • Med-QUEST has not made Hawaii's procedural-versus-ineligible Medicaid disenrollment split publicly available.
  • DHS BESSD does not publish monthly SNAP closure data by reason code, or 30/60/90-day reopening (churn) rates.
  • DHHL does not publish how many waitlist applicants die without a designated successor, or how many seats have been canceled under the 180-day rule.
  • The Hawaii Judiciary does not publish family-court self-represented-litigant rates by circuit, summary-possession default rates, or TRO petition disposition data.
  • The Crime Victim Compensation Commission's recent annual reports are published as image-only, non-machine-readable PDFs.
  • The Office of Elections does not publish AVR opt-in rates at DMV transactions or eligible-but-unregistered population by county.

Each of these is a forms problem with a forms answer. We're working on it.

It is not affiliated with the State of Hawaii, any county government, or any agency. Information is provided for navigation only and does not constitute legal, tax, or other professional advice. The case for why the project exists, with citations, is above in Why this matters.

What we do: point you to the agency's current page for each form, tell you the deadline, and tell you where to send it. What we don't do: host the PDFs ourselves, fill out the form for you, replace the official filing channel, or guarantee eligibility — always verify with the issuing agency before filing.

Why some links go to a PDF and some to an agency page: Hawaii agencies frequently rename PDF files when they revise them. A direct PDF link that worked last week may 404 this week. Where the agency provides a stable PDF URL (such as Maui County's DocumentCenter URLs, or DOTAX's /current/ aliases), the "Download the form PDF" button takes you straight there. Where the only available PDF has a year-tagged or hash-named URL that's likely to break on revision, the button instead reads "Find the form on the agency page" and takes you to the topical page where the current revision is always linked. The trade-off is one extra click, in exchange for a link that won't be broken in six months.

Coverage in v3: v1 covered the highest-value forms identified through a Hawaii civic-tech review — the four-county real-property exemptions, worker benefit forms (TDI, workers comp), key tax credits, cesspool conversion, and DHHL applications. v2 expanded to 138 forms across 14 categories. v3 brings the total to 158 forms across 14 categories, adding county camping permits, vehicle bill of sale, duplicate title / registration / driver license, notary commission, apostille, additional tax forms (L-72, D-100, N-356, N-200V), unemployment-insurance forms (UC-1, UC-25, UC-175, UC-253), articles of conversion, conveyance tax (P-64A/B), and a wage-garnishment release motion. v3 also introduces Walkthroughs — sequenced flows for life events (becoming a Hawaii resident, buying or selling a home, starting a business) that link into the catalog cards. Every form has been individually link-checked as of April 29, 2026 — URL, revision date, deadline, filing address, and current agency contact verified directly against the issuing agency. We're aiming for ~200 forms and 6+ walkthroughs in v4, with multilingual labels (Tagalog, Ilokano, Marshallese, Chuukese, ʻōlelo Hawaiʻi) to follow.

What we don't cover, and why: some categories of Hawaii civic life are better served by specialized nonprofits than by a forms-finder.
